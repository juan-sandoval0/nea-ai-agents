# NY DFS Opens Inquiry into Quantum Ledger's Blockchain Settlement Practices

**Source:** Bloomberg
**Date:** November 28, 2025
**Author:** Hannah Levitt

---

The New York Department of Financial Services has opened an inquiry into Quantum Ledger's use of blockchain technology for settling cross-border payments, according to two people familiar with the matter.

The inquiry, which began in early November, is focused on understanding how the fintech startup uses blockchain rails alongside traditional banking infrastructure, the people said. Regulators are particularly interested in the company's compliance frameworks for transactions that touch cryptocurrency networks.

Quantum Ledger, which has raised $78 million from investors including Ribbit Capital and Index Ventures, uses a hybrid settlement system that combines traditional correspondent banking with blockchain-based settlement to speed up cross-border B2B payments.

A spokesperson for Quantum Ledger confirmed the company had received an inquiry from NY DFS but characterized it as routine engagement with regulators.

"We proactively engage with our regulators and welcome opportunities to demonstrate our robust compliance framework," the spokesperson said. "We're confident in our approach and are cooperating fully with the DFS."

The inquiry comes amid broader regulatory scrutiny of the crypto and blockchain industry. While Quantum Ledger positions itself as a payments company rather than a crypto company, its use of blockchain technology for settlement has raised questions about which regulatory frameworks apply.

Industry observers note that the outcome of the inquiry could have implications beyond Quantum Ledger.

"There's a whole category of fintech companies using blockchain infrastructure behind the scenes," said a payments industry consultant who asked not to be named. "How regulators treat Quantum Ledger could set a precedent."

The company holds money transmitter licenses in 38 states and is authorized by the UK's Financial Conduct Authority. It is currently seeking authorization under the EU's Payment Services Directive.

Shares in publicly traded payment companies showed muted reaction to the news, suggesting markets view the inquiry as company-specific rather than industry-wide.
