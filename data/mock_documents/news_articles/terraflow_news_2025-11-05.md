# Data Center Operators Eye Geothermal as AI Power Demands Surge

**Source:** The Information
**Date:** November 5, 2025
**Author:** Julia Love

---

As artificial intelligence workloads drive unprecedented demand for data center power, some of the industry's largest operators are exploring geothermal energy as a solution—and early-stage startups are racing to meet the need.

TerraFlow Energy, a seed-stage geothermal company based in Denver, has signed a letter of intent with a major data center operator to explore on-site geothermal generation for a planned hyperscale facility, according to people familiar with the matter.

The LOI, while non-binding, signals growing interest from the data center industry in 24/7 clean energy sources that can provide the reliable baseload power AI workloads require. Unlike solar and wind, geothermal generates power continuously regardless of weather conditions.

"Data centers need power that's always on," said Dr. Maria Santos, TerraFlow's CEO, who confirmed the company has received interest from data center operators but declined to identify specific partners. "Geothermal is the only renewable source that can truly deliver that."

The potential deal would involve TerraFlow developing a geothermal installation adjacent to or integrated with a data center campus, providing dedicated clean power. One person familiar with the discussions suggested the installation could be sized at 50-100 MW—significant scale for an early-stage company.

Data centers have emerged as a major driver of energy demand, with AI training and inference particularly power-intensive. A single large language model training run can consume as much electricity as 100 homes use in a year, according to industry estimates.

Major operators including Google, Microsoft, and Amazon have made commitments to run their data centers on 24/7 carbon-free energy, creating demand for technologies like geothermal that can provide round-the-clock clean power.

Google has already partnered with TerraFlow competitor Fervo Energy for geothermal power, with a 115 MW project announced last year. Microsoft has expressed interest in next-generation geothermal as part of its carbon-negative goals.

For TerraFlow, a data center partnership could provide both a committed customer and a pathway to proving its technology at commercial scale. However, the company must first successfully complete its pilot demonstration, planned for 2026.

"The commercial interest is real and growing," said one climate tech investor. "The question for these early-stage companies is whether they can execute fast enough to capture it."
